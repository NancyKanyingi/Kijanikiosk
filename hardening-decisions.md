# KijaniKiosk Production Server Foundation: Hardening Decisions

The purpose of this foundation build is to establish a secure baseline for the KijaniKiosk production environment before application workloads are deployed. The objective is not simply to make the server operational, but to reduce the likelihood that a single mistake, software defect, or unauthorized action can compromise business operations. The controls selected focus on limiting access, reducing unnecessary privileges, protecting service availability, improving accountability, and supporting future compliance requirements.

A key design principle throughout this work is that every service should have access only to the resources required to perform its function and nothing more. This reduces the potential impact of accidental misuse, configuration errors, or malicious activity. If one component experiences a failure or compromise, the controls are designed to limit how far that issue can spread across the environment.

Another important principle is operational consistency. Production systems become difficult to secure when they rely on manual configuration or undocumented changes. The foundation therefore applies the same configuration repeatedly and verifies that the expected state has been achieved. This reduces operational risk and makes future audits easier to perform.

Visibility and accountability were also considered throughout the design. Security controls are significantly more effective when administrators can determine what happened, when it happened, and which service was involved. Logging, monitoring, and verification mechanisms were included to support incident investigation and operational troubleshooting without requiring emergency reconfiguration during an outage.

The following table summarizes the major controls implemented and the risks they are intended to address.

| Control                       | What it does                                                                                  | Risk mitigated                                                                                 |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Dedicated Service Accounts    | Separates application functions so each service operates independently with its own identity. | Prevents one compromised service from automatically gaining the privileges of another service. |
| Principle of Least Privilege  | Limits access rights to only those resources required for normal operation.                   | Reduces the damage that can occur if an account or service is misused.                         |
| Access Control Lists (ACLs)   | Provides controlled sharing of resources between authorized services.                         | Prevents unauthorized access while allowing legitimate operational collaboration.              |
| ProtectSystem=strict          | Restricts modification of protected system resources.                                         | Reduces the likelihood of unauthorized changes affecting system integrity.                     |
| NoNewPrivileges=true          | Prevents processes from gaining additional privileges after startup.                          | Limits escalation opportunities following a compromise.                                        |
| PrivateTmp=true               | Isolates temporary working areas used by individual services.                                 | Prevents services from interfering with one another through shared temporary data.             |
| UFW Network Segmentation      | Restricts network access to approved sources and approved services only.                      | Reduces exposure to unauthorized network activity and external attack attempts.                |
| Persistent Journaling         | Retains operational records across system restarts.                                           | Improves accountability and supports forensic investigation after incidents.                   |
| Log Rotation                  | Manages long-term growth of operational logs while preserving historical records.             | Prevents uncontrolled storage consumption from affecting service availability.                 |
| Health Monitoring             | Continuously validates service availability and records status information.                   | Improves detection of failures before they become business-impacting incidents.                |
| Service Isolation Controls    | Separates service execution environments from one another and from sensitive resources.       | Limits lateral movement opportunities if a service is compromised.                             |
| Automated Verification Checks | Confirms that required security controls remain in the expected state after deployment.       | Reduces the risk of configuration drift and unnoticed security gaps.                           |

The combination of these controls creates several layers of protection rather than relying on a single defensive measure. Network restrictions reduce unnecessary exposure. Access restrictions limit who can reach critical services. Service isolation limits the consequences of a successful compromise. Monitoring and logging improve detection and response capabilities. Verification procedures provide confidence that the intended protections remain active after deployment.

From a business perspective, these measures primarily support three outcomes. First, they reduce the likelihood of operational disruption caused by accidental changes or unauthorized activity. Second, they improve the organization's ability to investigate and respond to incidents through retained records and monitoring data. Third, they provide a stronger foundation for future compliance and governance activities by establishing consistent, repeatable controls that can be reviewed and audited.

The hardening effort also considered maintainability. Security controls that are too restrictive often create operational workarounds that eventually weaken security. For that reason, every control was tested to ensure that services remained functional while benefiting from additional protection. The objective was not to achieve the highest possible restriction level, but to achieve an appropriate balance between security, reliability, and operational practicality.

An additional consideration was resilience against configuration drift. Over time, production environments tend to accumulate manual changes that make systems harder to understand and secure. By defining the desired state and verifying it after deployment, the foundation reduces reliance on individual administrator knowledge and improves consistency across future deployments. This approach lowers operational risk and supports long-term scalability as the platform grows.

This foundation does not eliminate all risk. It does not protect against vulnerabilities that exist within the application code itself, misuse of valid credentials by authorized individuals, sophisticated attacks originating from trusted network locations, or failures caused by external service providers. It also cannot prevent business process errors, inadequate security awareness, or future configuration changes made outside established procedures. These risks require additional controls including secure software development practices, identity management, employee training, change management processes, regular vulnerability assessments, and ongoing security monitoring. The current foundation should therefore be viewed as a strong baseline rather than a complete security solution.
